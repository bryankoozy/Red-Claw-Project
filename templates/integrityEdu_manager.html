Leadership Commitment

Definition

Leadership commitment refers to a visible and proactive stance taken by top-level management (e.g., CEO, board of directors) declaring the company's opposition to corruption.

Importance

Leadership commitment shapes an ethics-oriented workplace culture. Senior managers are often perceived as role models, and employees model behavior based on top-level actions. This signals accountability and boosts confidence among investors and stakeholders.

How to Implement

  • Consistent Communication: Anti-corruption policy signed and published on intranet, employee handbooks, and onboarding packs
  • Annual Statements: Regular ethical business communications
  • Display of Commitment: Engagement in ethics talks, training townhalls, and anti-corruption pledges

Case Example: Keppel Corporation

Following a global corruption probe, Keppel strengthened its ethics governance by issuing CEO-led anti-corruption pledges and embedding them into board responsibilities.

Anti-Corruption Policy

Definition

A written document that declares your company's position on corruption, outlines prohibited practices, and guides legal and ethical conduct.

The 3 Ds Framework

  • Define: Clearly outline prohibited practices (bribery, facilitation payments, conflicts of interest)
  • Dedicate: Make formal commitments to ethical conduct and support whistleblowers
  • Document: Record and formalize actions, responsibilities, and compliance

Implementation Steps

  • Leadership endorsement via townhalls and communications
  • Ensure accessibility through multiple channels and languages
  • Mandatory training and annual declarations
  • Set up secure whistleblowing channels

Case Example: DBS Bank

Maintains a published anti-corruption and bribery policy, requiring mandatory online certification for employees and vendors every 12 months.

Code of Conduct

What is it

A practical guide that outlines the ethical principles and standards expected of employees in day-to-day situations, including grey areas.

Key Areas to Address

  • Conflict of interest situations
  • Gifts and entertainment guidelines
  • Personal loans from clients/customers policies
  • Scenario-based guidance for unclear situations

Implementation Approach

  • Leadership endorsement during onboarding and townhalls
  • Integration into HR processes and performance reviews
  • Interactive scenario-based training workshops
  • Regular refresher training every 1-2 years

Case Example: Siemens AG

Developed a comprehensive Code of Conduct using interactive tools and simulations. Staff must undergo scenario-based training on bribery risks and compliance behavior.

Regular Risk Assessments

What is it

A structured process to identify, evaluate, and prioritize corruption risks within the organization's operations, business units, geographies, or transactions.

Key Risk Areas

  • Gift and entertainment practices
  • Conflict of interest situations
  • Contributions and sponsorships
  • Third-party relationships
  • Procurement processes

Implementation Process

  • Map all operations and identify high-risk areas
  • Evaluate likelihood and impact of potential misconduct
  • Conduct risk workshops with vulnerable departments
  • Use checklists or heat maps to visualize risk exposure
  • Update assessments annually or upon major changes

Risk Mitigation Policies

What is it

Formal internal guidelines that proactively identify, control, and reduce specific corruption-related risks such as undue influence, improper benefits, or biased decision-making.

Key Policy Areas

  • Gifts & Entertainment: Define acceptable practices, set monetary thresholds, establish approval workflows
  • Conflict of Interest: Require disclosure of relationships that could impair objectivity
  • Contributions & Sponsorships: Create systems to log and review all external transactions

Implementation Strategy

  • Include sub-policies within overarching Anti-Corruption Policy
  • Automate approvals via compliance software
  • Conduct regular internal audits
  • Train staff with real-life examples
  • Assign compliance officer for oversight

Internal Controls

What is it

A set of procedures and approval structures designed to prevent, detect, and respond to corruption risks within day-to-day operations.

Key Control Principles

  • Clear internal control procedures for vulnerable areas
  • Segregation of duties - no single individual controls entire processes
  • Authorization protocols with dual sign-offs
  • Proper documentation and audit trails

Implementation Approach

  • Design and document SOPs for core business processes
  • Assign different roles: requestor, verifier, and approver
  • Configure systems to enforce approval hierarchies
  • Conduct periodic internal audits and control testing

Whistleblowing System

What is it

A formal mechanism that allows employees, vendors, or other stakeholders to report suspected corruption or unethical behavior confidentially and without fear of retaliation.

Key Components

  • Clear whistleblowing policies and procedures
  • Secure and accessible reporting channels (hotline, email, web portal)
  • Protection processes against retaliation
  • Prompt review and action procedures

Implementation Best Practices

  • Engage third-party providers for neutrality and anonymity
  • Train employees on reporting procedures and protections
  • Publicize channels through internal communications
  • Assign independent ethics team to manage reports

Periodic Monitoring & Improvement

What is it

The ongoing process of evaluating the effectiveness of anti-corruption measures, identifying gaps, and refining controls and policies to stay relevant and responsive to emerging risks.

Key Activities

  • Establish routine review schedules for controls and policies
  • Use feedback from audits, whistleblowing cases, and employee input
  • Monitor implementation progress and update stakeholders
  • Benchmark against industry best practices

Implementation Framework

  • Assign responsibility to compliance officer or ethics committee
  • Set clear KPIs: training completion rates, audit findings, hotline usage
  • Conduct periodic risk assessments for new corruption-prone areas
  • Revise policies based on review outcomes and communicate updates
